Detailed Project Explanation

Impact of COVID-19 on Maternal & Newborn Health

This project analyzes maternal mental health and neonatal outcomes during the COVID-19 pandemic using data from the Pregnancy during the COVID-19 Pandemic study conducted in Canada.

What The Project Talks About

Maternal stress, anxiety, and newborn outcomes during COVID-19.

The project studies how pregnancy during the pandemic was connected with mental health indicators and birth outcomes. It looks at maternal age, household income, education level, EPDS depression severity, PROMIS anxiety, pandemic stress, gestational age, birth weight, delivery mode, and NICU admission.

The final output is a dashboard-based descriptive analysis. Instead of building a prediction model, the goal is to make the dataset easier to understand through KPIs, segmentation, pivot tables, and interactive filters.

01

Data Cleaning

Removed unknown and incomplete records, handled missing numeric values with median or mode logic, reviewed extreme birth weight and gestational age values, and removed the delivery date column.

02

Feature Engineering

Created age category, depression severity, preterm status, income group, and maternal pandemic stress fields to make the dataset more useful for analysis.

03

Dashboard Design

Built scorecards, charts, slicers, and pivot-based views in Google Sheets so users can analyze outcomes by demographic and health factors.

Dashboard

How the project is showcased visually.

The dashboard includes KPI scorecards, preterm distribution by age category, income group distribution, maternal education by income group, depression severity by income, and mental health by education.

Key Insights

What the analysis found.

  • Anxiety and pandemic stress levels were elevated across the study population.
  • The preterm birth rate in the cleaned dataset was 19.4%.
  • Young mothers under 25 and high-risk mothers over 35 showed relatively higher preterm proportions.
  • Lower-income groups showed a higher proportion of severe depression cases.
  • C-section deliveries showed higher NICU admission rates compared with vaginal deliveries.
  • Mothers reported greater concern for their unborn baby than for their own health.

Recommendations

Routine EPDS and anxiety screening, targeted support for lower-income groups, age-based prenatal risk flagging, and psychosocial stress assessment during prenatal intake.

Limitations

The analysis has data attrition after removing unknown values, self-reported birth outcomes, approximate income midpoint conversion, and no post-pandemic comparison cohort.
